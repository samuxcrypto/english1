Direct & Indirect Speech

Reported speech conveys meaning without quoting exact words

Backshift of Tenses

When the reporting verb is past (said, told, asked), the original tense shifts one step back.

Direct SpeechReported SpeechExample
Present SimplePast Simple"I play cricket." → He said he played cricket.
Present ContinuousPast Continuous"I am studying." → She said she was studying.
Present PerfectPast Perfect"I have finished." → He said he had finished.
Past SimplePast Perfect"I visited Lahore." → She said she had visited Lahore.
Future (will)Would"I will call you." → He said he would call me.
CanCould"I can swim." → She said she could swim.
MayMight"I may come." → He said he might come.
Must (obligation)Had to"I must leave." → She said she had to leave.
Important Exception Universal truths and scientific facts do not need backshift.
The teacher said that the Earth revolves around the Sun. ✓

Say vs. Tell

Tell Needs a personal object → She told me that she was tired.
Say No object needed → She said (to me) that she was tired.

Reporting Questions

Yes/No questions use if / whether with statement word order (no inversion, no ?).

Direct"Are you coming to the party?" she asked me.
IndirectShe asked me if/whether I was coming to the party.

Wh- questions keep the question word but switch to statement order.

Direct"Where do you live?" he asked her.
IndirectHe asked her where she lived.

Commands & Requests

Use to + verb for positive and not to + verb for negative commands.

Direct"Don't touch that wire!" the electrician warned.
IndirectThe electrician warned them not to touch that wire.
🔎 Practice — Convert to Indirect Speech
  1. "I have already submitted my assignment," said Ali.
  2. "Can you close the window?" she asked me.
  3. "Don't be late tomorrow," the manager told the staff.
  4. "Where were you born?" the interviewer asked the candidate.
02

Conditional Sentences

Four types — from always-true facts to unreal past regrets

Zero Conditional — General Truths

If + Present Simple, Present Simple

ExamplesIf you heat ice, it melts.  |  If you mix blue and yellow, you get green.

First Conditional — Likely Future

If + Present Simple, will + base verb

ExamplesIf it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.  |  If you study hard, you will pass.

Second Conditional — Unreal Present / Future

If + Past Simple, would + base verb

Formal Note — Subjunctive Mood Use were for all subjects in second conditionals.
"If I were you, I would apologize." — NOT "If I was you"
ExamplesIf I had a million rupees, I would buy a house.  |  What would you do if you saw a ghost?

Third Conditional — Unreal Past (Regrets)

If + Past Perfect, would have + past participle

ExamplesIf you had studied harder, you would have passed.  |  If she had left earlier, she would not have missed the flight.

Quick Comparison

TypeIf ClauseMain ClauseMeaning

FirstPresent Simplewill + verbLikely future

🔎 Practice — Fill in the Blanks
  1. If she ______ (study) medicine, she ______ (become) a doctor by now. (third)
  2. If you heat metal, it ______ (expand). (zero)
  3. If I ______ (win) the lottery, I ______ (travel) the world. (second)

Essay Writing

Seven essay types and paragraph structure

The Seven Essay Types

TypePurposeExample Theme
NarrativeTells a story (real or imagined)"A Day I Will Never Forget"
DescriptiveVivid picture using sensory details"The Beauty of Northern Pakistan"
ReflectiveLooks back, analyzes personal growth"What I Learned from Failure"
ExpositoryExplains / informs factually"How the Internet Works"
ArgumentativeTakes a stance, uses evidence to persuade"Should Social Media Be Regulated?"
ImaginativeExplores unexperienced scenarios"A Day in the Life of an Astronaut"
ProcessStep-by-step explanation"How Tea Is Made"

Structuring a Strong Paragraph

The Four Parts 1. Topic Sentence — states the main idea.
2. Supporting Sentences — develop the idea with reasoning.
3. Details / Examples — statistics, real examples, comparisons.
4. Concluding Sentence — wraps up or bridges to next paragraph.

Full Essay Structure

Introduction: Hook + background + thesis statement.

Body Paragraphs (2–4): Each with its own topic sentence, evidence, and examples.

Conclusion: Restate thesis in new words + summarize + closing thought.

Write a topic sentence and two supporting sentences for an argumentative essay on: "Should mobile phones be allowed in schools?"

Reading Comprehension

Core strategies and question types

Three Core Strategies

StrategyWhen to UseHow
SkimmingGetting the general ideaRead quickly — don't read every word
ScanningFinding specific facts / names / datesLook for keywords without reading everything
InferencingReading between the linesUse tone, word choice, and context clues
Inference Example If the passage says "He slammed the door and refused to speak," you can infer the character is angry — even though "angry" is never written.

Question Types to Expect

  • Main Idea: "What is the passage mainly about?" → Skim first.
  • Detail: "According to the passage…" → Scan for keywords.
  • Inference: "What can be inferred…?" → Read between the lines.
  • Vocabulary in Context: Look at surrounding sentences, not just the dictionary.
  • Purpose / Tone: Is the writer critical, humorous, or neutral?
Exam Tip Read the questions before the passage — this tells you exactly what to scan for.

Punctuation

Marks, functions, and common mistakes

MarkFunctionExample
Full Stop (.)Ends a statementShe went to the market.
Question Mark (?)Ends a direct questionDid you finish your homework?
Exclamation Mark (!)Strong emotionWhat a beautiful sunset!
Semicolon (;)Joins two related independent clausesI studied all night; I still felt nervous.
Colon (:)Introduces a list or elaborationShe packed three items: a laptop, charger, and notebook.
Comma (,)Lists; clauses; after introductory wordsAlthough it was raining, we went outside.
Quotation Marks (" ")Direct speech or titlesThe teacher said, "Submit by Friday."
Apostrophe (')Possession or contractionAli's book; don't
Capital LettersSentence start; "I"; proper nouns; titlesOn Monday, I read The Alchemist.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

✗ Wrong
I was tired, I went to sleep. (comma splice)
✓ Correct
I was tired, so I went to sleep. or use a semicolon
✗ Wrong
My favorite subjects are: Math and English.
✓ Correct
I enjoy the following subjects: Math and English.

Email & Summary Writing

Formal vs. informal tone and concise summarisation

Formal vs. Informal Email

FeatureFormalInformal
Greeting"Dear Sir/Madam," or "Dear Mr. Khan,""Hi Ahmed," or "Hey!"
ToneProfessional, respectfulCasual, friendly
ContractionsAvoided ("do not")Common ("don't," "can't")
Vocabulary"I would like to request…""Can you…?"
Closing"Yours sincerely," / "Best regards,""Talk soon," / "Cheers,"

Summary Writing — Key Principles

  • Paraphrase, don't copy — rewrite ideas in your own words.
  • Keep it brief — about 1/4 to 1/3 the length of the original.
  • Main points only — leave out examples, repetition, minor details.
  • Maintain original meaning — no personal opinions or new information.
Useful Linking Phrases "The passage explains that…"  |  "The author argues that…"  |  "According to the text…"
